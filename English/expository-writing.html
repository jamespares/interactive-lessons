KS2–KS3 Writing Expository Writing Unit 2

Expository Writing — Interactive Lesson

Objective: Understand expository writing, distinguish it from creative and opinion writing, master personal vs impersonal expository styles, and practice factual writing.

1) Three Types of Writing

Creative

Purpose: To entertain and engage imagination

Features: Stories, descriptions, dialogue, emotions

Example: "The dragon swooped down, its scales glittering like emeralds in the moonlight."

Opinion

Purpose: To persuade or express beliefs

Features: I think, should, best, worse, personal views

Example: "I think pizza is the best food because it tastes delicious and everyone loves it."

Expository

Purpose: To inform and explain facts

Features: Facts only, clear structure, no opinions

Example: "Pizza originated in Naples, Italy. It typically consists of a wheat-based dough topped with tomato sauce and cheese."

Key Point: Expository writing is purely factual. No "I think", no "should", no opinions — just facts!

2) Personal vs Impersonal Expository Writing

Unlike creative and opinion writing, expository writing can be written in either first person (personal) or third person (impersonal), depending on what you're explaining.

Personal Expository (First Person)

When to use: Explaining about YOUR culture, YOUR tradition, YOUR experiences

Can use: I, we, my, our

Example:

"In my culture, we celebrate Chinese New Year. My family cleans the house before the festival. We eat dumplings together on New Year's Eve."

Notice: All facts about personal experience, no opinions!

Impersonal Expository (Third Person)

When to use: Explaining general facts, scientific information, historical events

Can use: It, they, the, people

Example:

"Chinese New Year is celebrated across Asia. People clean their homes before the festival. Families traditionally eat dumplings on New Year's Eve."

Notice: General facts, no personal reference!

Important: If a question asks "Explain about YOUR culture" or "YOUR tradition" → Use personal expository (I, we, my).

Important: If a question asks "Explain how photosynthesis works" → Use impersonal expository (it, they, plants).

3) Expository Structure: Summary, Not Conclusion

Creative and opinion writing have conclusions where you express final thoughts or feelings. Expository writing ends with a summary that restates the key facts.

Writing Type Ending Example
Creative Conclusion with emotion "And they lived happily ever after."
Opinion Conclusion with persuasion "Therefore, schools should ban homework."
Expository Summary of facts "In summary, photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy."

Sample Expository Paragraph Structure:

1. Topic sentence (introduces the topic)

2. Supporting facts (3-5 facts with details)

3. Summary sentence (restates main facts)

Remember: No opinions, no "I think", no "should" — only facts!

7) Spot the Mistake: Find the Non-Factual Words

Each paragraph below LOOKS like expository writing but contains opinion words. Click the words that don't belong in expository writing.

Paragraph 1:

The Great Wall of China is amazing. It stretches over 21,000 kilometers. Construction began in the 7th century BC. The wall was built to protect Chinese states from invasions. Today, it should be visited by everyone because it's the best historical site.

Correct answers: amazing, should, the best (all opinion words)

Paragraph 2:

In my family, we celebrate Mid-Autumn Festival every year. We eat mooncakes, which taste delicious. The festival falls on the 15th day of the 8th lunar month. My grandmother makes lanterns, and I think this is the most fun part of the celebration.

Correct answers: taste delicious, I think, the most fun

8) Writing Practice: Personal Expository Paragraph

Prompt: Explain about a tradition your family celebrates. Remember to:

  • Use personal pronouns (I, we, my, our)
  • State only facts (no "I think", no "should")
  • End with a summary, not a conclusion
Model paragraph

"In my family, we celebrate Chinese New Year every January or February. My parents clean the house thoroughly before the festival begins. We decorate our home with red lanterns and couplets. On New Year's Eve, my whole family gathers for a large dinner. We eat dumplings, fish, and other traditional foods. Children receive red envelopes with money from elders. The celebration lasts for fifteen days. In summary, Chinese New Year is our most important family tradition."

9) Writing Practice: Impersonal Expository Paragraph

Prompt: Explain how plants make food (photosynthesis). Remember to:

  • Use impersonal language (it, they, plants)
  • State only facts
  • End with a summary
Model paragraph

"Plants make food through a process called photosynthesis. Chlorophyll in the leaves absorbs sunlight. The plant takes in carbon dioxide from the air through tiny pores. Water is absorbed by the roots from the soil. The plant combines these elements to produce glucose, a type of sugar. Oxygen is released as a byproduct. This process occurs mainly in the leaves. In summary, photosynthesis allows plants to convert light energy into chemical energy for growth."

11) Lesson Summary

Key Takeaways:

  • Expository writing is purely factual — no opinions!
  • Use personal expository (I, we, my) when writing about YOUR culture or YOUR experiences
  • Use impersonal expository (it, they, the) for general facts and scientific explanations
  • Expository writing ends with a summary, not a conclusion
  • Avoid opinion words: "I think", "should", "best", "worst", emotional language
  • Stick to facts that can be verified or proven

Checklist for Expository Writing:

  • ✓ Topic sentence introduces the topic clearly
  • ✓ 3-5 supporting facts with details
  • ✓ No opinion words or "I think" statements (unless it's personal expository using "I" for facts)
  • ✓ Clear, factual language throughout
  • ✓ Summary sentence restates the main facts
  • ✓ Appropriate use of personal or impersonal voice based on the question
